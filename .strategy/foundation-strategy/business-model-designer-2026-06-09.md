## Executive Summary

1:90 Align is a pre-revenue, solo-founded, bootstrapped business attempting to launch **four product lines simultaneously** — a methodology (the Arc → Wave → Milestone → Task framework), a SaaS platform, training programmes, and a coach certification — positioned as "the UK alternative to EOS." The ambition is sound and the framework is genuinely differentiated, but the model as currently scoped is **structurally over-extended for a solo bootstrapper**: each of the four lines is a different business with different economics, cost bases, and time-to-revenue, and none has been chosen as the wedge.

The single highest-leverage decision is **sequencing**: which line earns cash now, which builds the moat, and which comes last. The proven path — the one EOS itself actually used — is **certification-and-licensing-led, not software-led**. EOS Worldwide is fundamentally a certification, membership, book, and tools business; the software (Ninety.io) came later and is a separate company. A solo bootstrapper leading with SaaS is the highest-cost, slowest, most capital-hungry route available.

**Revenue Model**: Hybrid (services now → recurring licensing/certification → SaaS later)
**Primary Segment**: UK SME owner-operators (10–250 staff), £1M–£25M revenue, stuck between strategy and execution
**LTV:CAC Ratio**: Not yet measurable (pre-revenue) — modelled scenarios below
**Break-Even Timeline**: 6–12 months achievable IF focused on services-led entry; 24–36+ months if SaaS-led
**Strategic Coherence**: **Medium-Low** — strong value prop, but product sprawl and an unfunded SaaS ambition undermine focus
**Business Model Viability**: **Medium** — high potential, currently diluted by lack of focus and sequencing

## 1. Customer Segments

**Primary Segment — UK SME owner-operators**
- **Demographics**: Companies of 10–250 employees, £1M–£25M turnover, UK-based, founder/owner-led. Sectors where execution discipline is weak but ambition is high: professional services, agencies, construction/trades, light manufacturing, fast-growing service businesses.
- **Psychographics**: The owner is the bottleneck. They have a vision but the team doesn't share or execute it. They've tried task tools (Asana/Monday) and they haven't fixed the underlying problem. They may have heard of EOS/Traction but found it very US-flavoured.
- **Buying behaviour**: The owner *is* the budget authority — short, relationship-led sales cycles. They buy on trust and a credible diagnosis of their pain, not on feature lists. They respond to "I've sat where you're sitting" (Phil's operator background is a genuine asset here).

**Secondary Segment — Business coaches & independent consultants**
- People who advise SMEs and want a **branded, structured methodology** to deliver (rather than improvising). This is the segment that turns 1:90 Align from a consultancy into a *scalable* business — they become the distribution channel. This mirrors exactly how EOS scaled via certified EOS Implementers.

**Segmentation Strategy**: Currently **diversified by accident** (four products implicitly chase different buyers). The recommendation is a **deliberate two-sided sequence**: win SME owners first (proves the method, generates case studies and cash), then recruit coaches to scale delivery (turns proof into leverage).

**Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — for the entry wedge:
- **Title**: Founder / Managing Director / CEO
- **Company size**: 15–80 employees, £2M–£15M revenue
- **Geography**: UK (regional — not just London; the "UK-native, not a US import" angle plays well outside the M25)
- **Pain**: "My team is busy but we're not moving the needle on the things that matter. Annual plans die by March."
- **Buying behaviour**: Books a call after reading a blog post or being referred; decides within 1–3 conversations.

## 2. Value Propositions

**For UK SME owner-operators who are stuck between strategy and daily execution, 1:90 Align is a complete business operating system that turns one 90-day priority into aligned action across the whole team. Unlike EOS/Traction (US-built, consultant-gated) or task tools (Asana/Monday, which organise work but don't connect it to strategy), 1:90 Align is UK-native, framework-first, and designed so a small business can run it without a permanent consultant.**

**Value delivered (claimed — see risk note):**
- Focus: one strategic Arc per 90 days replaces scattered priorities
- Alignment: every person can see how today's task ladders up to the quarter's goal
- Rhythm: a repeatable cadence (Waves, Milestones, Ripples) so plans don't die after kickoff

**Value type**: Primarily **Performance + Convenience** (better execution, in a system that's simpler and more UK-relevant than the alternatives).

**Differentiation that is real and defensible:**
1. **UK-native positioning** — genuinely open white space; EOS/Scaling Up/Ninety.io are all US-first.
2. **Founder authenticity** — Phil's operator-to-MBA-to-PLC path is a credibility asset US franchises can't replicate locally.
3. **"One vision, 90 days"** — the naming encodes the method; tighter and more memorable than "EOS."

> ⚠️ **CREDIBILITY RISK (act on this):** The live homepage currently states "**100+ UK Companies**" and "**40% Faster Delivery**." For a pre-revenue business these are unsubstantiated and create real legal (ASA/CAP Code misleading-advertising) and trust risk — a savvy SME buyer or competitor will check. **Recommendation:** replace with honest, equally-persuasive framing that doesn't claim a customer base you don't yet have, e.g. lead with the *method* and the *founder's track record* ("Built from 20 years systemising operations inside a UK PLC") rather than fabricated client counts. This should be fixed on the site before any paid traffic is sent to it.

## 3. Channels

### Customer Journey Map
- **Awareness**: Organic content/SEO (already built — 9 blog posts + cluster strategy), founder's LinkedIn/network, referrals. *This is the bootstrapper's correct primary channel — owned, compounding, near-zero cash cost.*
- **Evaluation**: The "book a free call" consult, the comparison page, case studies (none yet — this is a gap to fill with the first 3–5 clients).
- **Purchase**: Founder-led conversation today. No self-service purchase path (correct for a high-trust services sale).
- **Delivery**: In-person/remote facilitation (services); the platform (software) later.
- **After-sales**: Direct from Phil now; community + certified-coach support later.

**Channel strategy**: **Direct + content-led now**, evolving to **indirect (certified coaches as a channel)** once the method is proven. The certified-coach network is the channel innovation that breaks the founder-time ceiling.

## 4. Customer Relationships

- **Now**: **Personal assistance** — high-touch, founder-delivered. Right for early trust-building and learning, wrong for scale.
- **Acquisition**: Content → call → diagnosis → paid engagement.
- **Retention**: The 90-day cycle is a natural recurring touchpoint — each quarter is a renewal moment. This is a structural retention advantage if productised as an ongoing programme rather than a one-off.
- **Upsell path**: One-off implementation → ongoing quarterly facilitation → software seat expansion → (for coaches) certification.
- **Expected lifetime**: If sold as a quarterly programme, 4–8 quarters (12–24 months) is realistic for SME engagements.

### Recommended revenue sequence (the core strategic recommendation)

**Phase 1 (Months 0–9) — Services-led, cash-positive:**
Productise Phil's time into a fixed-scope offer. Indicative pricing (to be validated by the pricing skill):
- *90-Day Kickstart* (one Arc, set up + facilitated): **£3,000–£6,000** one-off
- *Quarterly Operating Partner* (ongoing facilitation): **£1,200–£2,500/quarter** recurring
- Goal: 5–10 paying SMEs → first case studies → break-even on founder living costs.

**Phase 2 (Months 9–24) — Certification = leverage:**
Package the method into a certification for coaches/consultants. This is how EOS makes most of its money.
- *Certification fee*: **£4,000–£9,000** per coach (one-off)
- *Annual licence/membership*: **£1,000–£3,000/yr** recurring (tools, brand, community, leads)
- This converts Phil's time-bound consultancy into a **royalty-style recurring base** that scales with the network, not his hours.

**Phase 3 (Months 24+) — SaaS, once there's a base to sell into:**
Only build/scale the platform when there are certified coaches + clients who *need* it to run the method. Then it's a high-margin add-on with a built-in install base — not a cold, capital-burning bet.
- *SaaS*: **£15–£40/user/month** or a per-company plan.

### Unit economics (modelled — pre-revenue, illustrative)

*Services-led entry, conservative:*
- ARPU (Quarterly Operating Partner @ £1,800/qtr × 4): **~£7,200/yr**
- CAC (content + founder time, low cash): **~£300–£800** effective
- Gross margin (services): ~70% (it's largely Phil's time)
- LTV (5 quarters avg × £1,800 × 0.7): **~£6,300**
- **LTV:CAC ≈ 8–20:1** on a content-led, referral-heavy motion — strong, *because* CAC is near-zero while bootstrapped. The constraint is founder *capacity*, not CAC.

*The capacity ceiling is the real economic limit of Phase 1* — which is exactly why Phase 2 (certification) matters: it lifts the ceiling.

## 9. Cost Structure

**Today (near-zero cash):** founder time + hosting (~£10–30/mo) + domain + tools. This is the bootstrapper's advantage — runway is effectively infinite as long as Phil's living costs are covered.

**The one cost that breaks the model:** committing to building/operating SaaS solo. That implies either Phil stops selling (no cash) to code, or hiring engineers (no capital). **Both are fatal in Phase 1.** Deferring SaaS to Phase 3 keeps the cost structure asset-light and survivable.

**Break-even**: Defined by Phil's personal income floor. At ~£1,800/qtr per client, **8–12 active quarterly clients ≈ a sustainable founder income** — achievable inside 6–12 months with focused selling. That's the real break-even target, not a SaaS MRR number.

**Cost structure type**: **Value-driven and deliberately lean** — keep cash costs near zero, invest founder *time* into the highest-leverage activity (selling + proof), defer all capital-hungry activities.

## Conclusion

1:90 Align has a **genuinely strong value proposition and a real, defensible white space** (UK-native business operating system). What it lacks is **focus and sequencing**. The business is currently scoped as if it were four funded companies; it is one solo bootstrapper.

The winning move is to stop thinking of the four products as a launch menu and start thinking of them as a **staircase**: services for cash and proof → certification for leverage and recurring revenue → software once there's an install base that needs it. This is, not coincidentally, the exact path EOS took — and it's the path that fits a bootstrapped founder's constraints.

**Business Model Viability: Medium** (High potential, currently diluted by lack of focus).

**Key strengths:**
1. Defensible UK-native positioning in a space dominated by US imports.
2. Founder credibility and authentic origin story.
3. Asset-light cost base = effectively unlimited runway while focused on services.

**Key risks:**
1. **Product sprawl** — trying to run four businesses at once as a solo founder.
2. **SaaS trap** — leading with the most capital-intensive line while bootstrapped.
3. **Unsubstantiated marketing claims** — live homepage stats create legal/trust exposure.

**Next steps:**
1. **Commit to services-led entry.** Productise the "90-Day Kickstart" + "Quarterly Operating Partner" offers and put a price on them.
2. **Fix the homepage claims** to honest, founder-led framing.
3. **Sell to the first 5–10 SMEs**, capture case studies, then design the certification programme (Phase 2).
4. **Freeze SaaS build** at maintenance until Phase 3.
