## Executive Summary

The competitive set divides into three camps: **operating-system frameworks** (EOS, Scaling Up — the real competitors), **goal frameworks** (OKRs — a foil to position against), and **task software** (Asana/Monday — not real competitors, but where buyers wrongly look). The most important finding: **EOS is not a software company — it is a certification-and-licensing business**, and its economics are public and excellent. That validates 1:90 Align's Phase-2 path and gives a proven pricing template. The UK is structurally under-served: EOS's certified-implementer network is US-dense and UK-thin, and every leading brand reads as American. **The competitive gap is real, large, and time-limited** — it will close as US players localise.

## Competitor Deep-Dives

### 1. EOS / Traction (the primary competitor)
- **What it is**: "Entrepreneurial Operating System" — Gino Wickman's *Traction* book + a network of certified/franchised Implementers + tools. The category-definer.
- **Business model (the key insight)**: Primarily **certification + franchise/licensing + book + tools** — *not* software. Public figures:
  - Implementers charge clients **$4,500–$6,600/session**, **~$36K–$52.8K in year one**, **$81K–$118K over a typical 36-month engagement** ([source](https://www.businessaction.com.au/why-eos-implementers-charge-different-rates-and-what-youre-really-paying-for/)).
  - Implementer franchise investment: **$61K–$151K**; **monthly royalty $1,195–$1,495** ([source](https://www.franchisepayback.com/franchise/eos)).
  - Average implementer location revenue: **~$368K/yr**.
- **Strengths**: Brand dominance, huge book reach, proven curriculum, large implementer army, network effects.
- **Weaknesses (1:90's openings)**: Unmistakably **US** in language/culture; **implementer-gated** (you largely need a paid implementer to do it well); **expensive** ($36K+/yr); franchise model is rigid/exclusive; thin, pricey UK coverage.
- **How to beat it**: Don't fight the brand. Win on **UK-native + accessible + operator-credible + lower entry price**. Target the buyer who finds EOS too American, too expensive, or can't get a good UK implementer.

### 2. Scaling Up (Rockefeller Habits — Verne Harnish)
- **What**: A comprehensive scaling methodology + coaches + book.
- **Strengths**: Deep, respected, strong for larger/ambitious firms.
- **Weaknesses**: Even more complex and enterprise/US-flavoured than EOS; heavier lift for a 20-person SME.
- **Opening**: 1:90's **simplicity** ("one Arc per 90 days") is the counter — Scaling Up can feel overwhelming; 1:90 is digestible.

### 3. OKRs (the foil, not a competitor)
- **What**: Objectives & Key Results — a goal-setting framework, not an operating system.
- **Strategic use**: **Position against it.** OKRs are famous for failing without an execution system around them. Your SEO cluster already targets "OKR alternative." Message: *"OKRs tell you what to aim at. 1:90 Align actually gets you there."*

### 4. Ninety.io (the EOS-adjacent software)
- **What**: SaaS built to run EOS. Notably, it's a **separate company** from EOS Worldwide — proof that even in the EOS world, framework and software are split.
- **Implication**: Reinforces that 1:90 should *not* try to be a software company first. If anything, Ninety.io is the cautionary tale for Phase 3 (well-funded, team-built — not a solo bootstrap target).

### 5. Asana / Monday / ClickUp (where buyers wrongly look)
- **What**: Work-management SaaS.
- **Reality**: Not real competitors — they organise tasks, they don't connect strategy to execution. But buyers *conflate* them with the category.
- **Move**: Explicitly **de-position** from them: "Not a task list. An operating system." (The site already says this — keep it.)

### 6. UK business coaching / ActionCOACH / independent advisors
- **What**: Generalist business coaching franchises and solo advisors.
- **Weakness**: Mostly *un-systematised* — coaching without a branded, repeatable method.
- **Opening**: This is 1:90's **Phase-2 recruiting pool** — give these advisors a method to license. ActionCOACH proves UK appetite for a franchised business-improvement model exists.

## Threats & Time Sensitivity

1. **EOS localises to the UK** — the biggest threat. EOS could recruit more UK implementers and soften its US feel. **Window: ~18–36 months.** Move fast on owning "UK business operating system" in search and mind-share.
2. **A UK competitor has the same idea** — the gap is visible; someone else could claim it. First-mover on content/SEO (already underway) is the defence.
3. **Buyer inertia** ("we have Monday / we do an annual offsite") — the perennial threat; defeated by sharp problem-led messaging, not competitor-bashing.

---

## What to Steal / Adapt from EOS

- **The certification-and-royalty model** — it's proven and lucrative ($368K avg/implementer; recurring royalties). Adapt it as 1:90's Phase-2 engine, but **less gated and lower entry price** to undercut.
- **The book-as-funnel** — *Traction* is EOS's #1 lead gen. **Phil should write the 1:90 Align book** (or a strong lead-magnet guide) — it's the cheapest, most durable authority + funnel asset a bootstrapper can build.
- **The "implementer" identity** — people *aspire* to become certified EOS Implementers. Create the equivalent aspirational identity for 1:90 certified coaches.
