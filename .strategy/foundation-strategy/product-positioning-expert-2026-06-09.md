## Executive Summary

1:90 Align's positioning opportunity is unusually clean: **it can credibly own "the UK business operating system"** — a category frame where every serious competitor (EOS/Traction, Scaling Up, Ninety.io) is a US import. The current site gestures at this ("UK alternative to EOS") but doesn't *commit* to it as the organising idea. The recommendation is to stop positioning against task tools (Asana/Monday — a crowded, losing frame) and position squarely as **the UK-native operating system for owner-led businesses**, with the four products framed as one system, not a menu.

**Recommended positioning statement:**
> *For UK business owners whose strategy never survives contact with daily work, 1:90 Align is the business operating system that turns one 90-day priority into aligned action across the whole team — built in the UK, for UK businesses, by an operator who's run one.*

## 1. Competitive Alternatives (what customers use today)

What does a stuck SME owner do *instead* of buying 1:90 Align?

| Alternative | What it is | Where it's weak |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **EOS / Traction** | The category leader; book + certified implementers | US-built language/culture; implementer-gated (expensive, scarce in UK); feels heavyweight |
| **Scaling Up (Rockefeller Habits)** | Verne Harnish's system | Even more US/enterprise-flavoured; complex |
| **OKRs** | Goal-setting framework | Just goals — no execution rhythm; notoriously fails without a system around it |
| **Task tools (Asana/Monday/ClickUp)** | Work management software | Organise tasks, but never connect them to strategy — the actual problem |
| **Do nothing / spreadsheets + annual offsite** | The real default | Plans die by Q2; the pain that creates the market |

**Strategic read:** The most dangerous competitor is not EOS — it's **"do nothing"** and **"we already have Monday."** Positioning must defeat *those* first.

## 2. Unique Attributes (what only 1:90 Align has)

1. **UK-native** — language, examples, business culture, pricing in £, no US franchise feel.
2. **A complete system, not a layer** — framework + delivery + (eventually) software + a coach network, vs point solutions.
3. **The 90-day "one Arc" constraint** — a sharper, more memorable focusing mechanism than EOS's broader toolkit.
4. **Operator-founder credibility** — Phil has actually run operations inside a UK PLC; not a career consultant.
5. **Un-gated entry** — designed so an SME can start without a scarce, expensive certified implementer.

## 3. Value (what those attributes let customers do)

- Get the whole team pulling toward *one* priority each quarter (focus).
- See strategy reach the frontline — the thing every other tool fails at (alignment).
- Run it themselves, affordably, without a US-style implementer contract (accessibility).

## 4. Target Market Characteristics (who cares most)

The buyers who value these attributes most: **UK owner-operators of 15–80-person businesses who have already felt the pain of a plan dying** — often after trying OKRs or a task tool and finding it didn't fix execution. Regional UK (not just London) is the sweet spot, where "US import fatigue" is highest and EOS implementer coverage is thinnest.

## 5. Market Category (the frame)

**Recommended frame: "Business Operating System" — owned with a UK modifier.**

- ✅ **"The UK business operating system"** — credible, defensible, differentiated.
- ❌ Avoid framing as "project management" or "planning software" — that drops you into the Asana/Monday bloodbath where you lose on features and budget.
- The category "business operating system" is *understood enough* (thanks to EOS popularising it) that you don't have to educate from zero, but *un-owned in the UK* — the ideal positioning condition.

## 6. Relevant Trends (tailwinds to ride)

- **"Buy British / support UK" sentiment** post-Brexit in B2B procurement.
- **Distrust of US SaaS data/pricing** (GDPR, £/$ swings) — a UK-native, UK-hosted system is a quiet trust signal.
- **OKR backlash** — growing "OKRs don't work" discourse creates demand for an alternative (your SEO cluster already targets this).
- **Fractional/advisory boom** — more independent advisors who could become certified deliverers.

## Positioning Moves (concrete)

**1. Rewrite the homepage hero around the category claim, not fabricated stats.**
Replace "100+ UK Companies / 40% faster" with the *real* differentiators:
> "The UK Business Operating System. One vision. 90 days. Your whole team aligned. Built by an operator who's run one — not a US franchise."

**2. Reframe the four products as one system, visually.**
Framework (the method) → Software (runs it) → Training (learn it) → Certification (deliver it). Show them as a stack, not a nav menu of separate things. One system, four ways in.

**3. Pick the enemy deliberately.** Position *against EOS's US-ness* and *against task-tools' shallowness* — not against everyone. "Not another US framework. Not another task list."

**4. Lead messaging with the founder.** In a trust-led SME sale, "built by an operator who's sat in your chair" beats any feature. Phil's story *is* the positioning.
