Goal Setting

SMART Goals: Definitions, Examples & a Better Way to Use Them

By Phil Martin | 23 June 2026 | 7 min read

A SMART goal is a goal written to a simple five-part standard so that it is clear, trackable and time-bound. The SMART acronym stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Time-bound. The method has been a fixture of management since the early 1980s for one good reason: it forces a vague intention like "grow the business" into something you can actually act on and measure.

This guide gives you the full definition of each part of the SMART framework, worked examples for real business situations, and a template you can use today. It also covers the part most articles skip — the limitation of SMART goals, and what to pair them with so they actually get delivered rather than forgotten by March.

The SMART Acronym, Defined

Each letter is a test your goal must pass. A goal that fails any one of them is not yet a SMART goal — it is still a wish.

S — Specific

The goal names exactly what will be achieved, with no room for interpretation. "Improve customer service" is not specific. "Reduce average first-response time on support tickets from 8 hours to 2 hours" is. A specific goal answers what, who and where.

M — Measurable

There is a number attached, so you can tell — objectively — whether you have hit it. If you cannot measure it, you cannot manage it, and you will argue forever about whether it was achieved. The measure is the difference between "more leads" and "120 qualified leads".

A — Achievable

The goal stretches the team but is realistic given your resources, time and starting point. Achievable does not mean easy — it means possible. A goal nobody believes in is quietly abandoned; a goal with no ambition changes nothing.

R — Relevant

The goal genuinely matters to the wider direction of the business right now. A goal can be perfectly specific and measurable and still be the wrong thing to chase this quarter. Relevant asks: if we achieve this, does it actually move the business forward?

T — Time-bound

There is a deadline. A goal without a date is a goal without urgency — it slides indefinitely. The deadline creates the pull that turns intention into action. "By 30 September" is a goal; "eventually" is not.

SMART Goals Examples

The fastest way to understand SMART goals is to see a weak goal rewritten as a SMART one. Here are examples across common business functions.

Weak goal SMART version
Grow salesIncrease monthly recurring revenue from £40k to £50k by 30 September by converting 15 trial accounts.
Improve onboardingCut new-client onboarding time from 3 weeks to 5 working days for the next 10 clients by end of Q3.
Hire more peopleRecruit and fully onboard two qualified delivery consultants by 31 August.
Reduce churnLower monthly customer churn from 4.5% to 3% within 90 days by launching a structured check-in at day 30.

A Free SMART Goals Template

Use this sentence template to draft any SMART goal. Fill in the brackets and you will pass all five tests in one line.

"We will [specific outcome] from [current measure] to [target measure] by [deadline], because [why it matters now]."

The "because" clause is the one most people leave off — but it is what makes the goal Relevant, and it is what keeps the team committed when the work gets hard.

The Limitation Nobody Mentions

Here is the honest truth about SMART goals: the framework only tells you how to phrase a goal. It says nothing about how to focus, how to sequence, or how to execute. And that is where goals actually die.

Most teams do not fail because their goals were badly worded. They fail because they set ten beautifully-worded SMART goals at once — ten competing priorities that quietly starve each other of attention. SMART has no opinion on how many goals you should have, so people set far too many. It has no opinion on cadence, so there is no rhythm of review. It has no opinion on ownership, so no single person is accountable. And it has no mechanism for connecting the goal on the slide to the work someone does on a Tuesday.

A SMART goal, in other words, is a well-written destination with no route, no vehicle and no schedule. Necessary — but nowhere near sufficient.

A Better Way: One SMART Goal Per 90 Days

The fix is not to abandon SMART — it is to apply it with ruthless focus and wrap it in a system that executes. That is the idea behind the 1:90 Align framework: instead of a long list of goals, you set one dominant priority — an Arc — for the next 90 days. The Arc is itself a textbook SMART goal: specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound to exactly one quarter.

The difference is everything SMART leaves out. The single-Arc rule forces the focus SMART never demanded — one priority, not ten. Then the framework adds the execution layer the SMART acronym is missing:

  • →Waves — three 30-day sprints break the 90-day goal into a rhythm of work, review and adjustment.
  • →Milestones — dated, owned proof-points within each Wave, so progress is visible weekly and a single person is accountable.
  • →Tasks — the daily work, each one linked up to a Milestone, so everyone can see how today connects to the quarter's goal.

A SMART goal tells you what good looks like. The Arc → Wave → Milestone → Task structure is how you actually get there. Pair the two and a goal stops being a January aspiration and becomes a September result.

SMART Goals: Frequently Asked Questions

What does SMART stand for in goal setting?

SMART stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Time-bound — a five-part checklist for writing a goal clearly enough that you will know exactly what success looks like and when it is due.

What is an example of a SMART goal?

A weak goal is "grow sales". The SMART version is "Increase monthly recurring revenue from £40,000 to £50,000 by 30 September by converting 15 trial accounts" — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound.

Are SMART goals still effective?

Yes — for phrasing a single goal clearly. But SMART only describes how to word a goal. It says nothing about focus, cadence, ownership or how the goal connects to daily work, which is where most goals actually fail.

How many SMART goals should you set?

Fewer than most teams think. A long list of SMART goals is still a list of competing priorities. Focusing on one dominant goal per 90-day cycle concentrates effort and is far more likely to be achieved.

What is the difference between SMART goals and OKRs?

A SMART goal is a way of phrasing one objective. OKRs pair an objective with measurable key results. Neither, on its own, provides the execution rhythm — the weekly checkpoints and daily work — needed to actually deliver the goal.
